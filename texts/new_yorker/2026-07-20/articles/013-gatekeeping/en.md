A few passersby pause to listen. A lady holds her toddler’s hand, wagging it along with the music. But Carl doesn’t pay any of us any mind.

Some birds glide above us, en route from one terminal to the next. They linger for a minute, circling Carl, and then they’re gone.

Eventually, the music stops.

Carl stands, slowly. The woman and her kid start to clap. I do, too. And Carl gives us a long look before taking a bow.

A few days later, a Black woman I’m wheeling to an Amsterdam flight is dozing in her chair when I ask if I can tell her a story.

Her braids are long, tucked under a knit cap. She looks up at me, squinting.

You didn’t see me sleeping? she asks.

You’re fine, she says, waving her hand. What’ve you got?

Once, I say, there was a boy who I worked with.

We worked together in the evening. Sometimes the airport was busy. But sometimes it wasn’t. On the empty days, we’d lie down on the floor and stare up at the sky through the window above.

But, I say, one day we found a bird in the terminal.

A bird, the woman says.

She nods for me to continue.

His wing was clipped, I say. I didn’t want to hold him, because of the possibility, you know, of disease. But my friend didn’t care about that. That didn’t matter to him. So he picked up the bird, and he asked me to call a car, and I did. We rode together to the vet clinic. It was, like, four in the morning. Behind the desk, a sleepy attendant eyed us. But then we were called into a room where the bird was checked out. We were told that the clinic would take care of him, but my friend asked if we could receive updates, so for the next few weeks this vet sent us videos of the bird. Moving tentatively. Flapping around. And then, like a month later, the bird was released.

We even named him, I say. But the thing is, I don’t remember what we decided on.

A crowd of young tennis players passes us, jostling and laughing. Their coaches try to corral them. When I look down at the woman, I see that she’s fallen back asleep.

Which is for the best, probably.

So I continue wheeling her to the gate. But then she reaches up, slowly, and rests a hand on mine, rubbing it with her thumb.

Once, a few years ago, I was walking back from a gate where I had dropped off a passenger when I saw a guy lying on the floor.

His arms and legs were splayed out. You see a lot of shit after midnight, but this guy had on a flight-attendant uniform. I thought about checking on him, but that didn’t seem worth it. Probably had his own shit going on.

Then I changed my mind. Turned around and walked back.

Standing over JT, I asked what was wrong.

I fucked up today, he said.

Cussed out a passenger.

Not really. No. But I think you’ll be fine.

Maybe, JT said. I also might lose my job.

O.K., I said. You’ll get another one.

You’re pretty confident, he said.

Trust me, I said. I’m not.

I sat beside JT. Don’t know why.

So you need to lie on the ground? I asked.

It helps me. Puts things in perspective. How often do you actually look up?

There isn’t much to see.

JT turned to me. He seemed genuinely curious. Then he patted the space beside him.

I frowned. But then, nearly involuntarily, I lay down, too. It really was the first time that I’d looked up at the airport.

But also, probably, the first time anywhere, in a long, long time.

Nothing to report, I said. But it did look a bit different.

O.K., JT said. But let’s stay a bit longer. Just to make sure.

The morning after JT returns to Taiwan, I’m lying on the sofa, dozing, when my brother opens the front door and steps out of his shoes.

He takes one look at me. Then he looks again.

You’re off today? he asks.

And you’re just spending it here?

Where else would I go?

My brother smirks at me. Stuffs his hands in the pocket of his hoodie.

What about that guy? he asks. Your boyf—

I don’t have one of those, I say.

My brother sighs. Then he walks into the kitchen, where he opens drawers and pulls a tub of cold rice from the fridge. He lines the counter with vegetables, condiments, and a plastic container of minced crab.

Eventually, my brother comes back into the living room and drops a bowl in front of me. It’s fried rice, topped with chile oil and scallions and fried shallots, stuffed with crab and carrots and eggs and onions and peas and herbs. He settles across from me, cross-legged, sipping from a beer can and picking at a bowl of nuts.

We sit in front of the television. Leopards chase a gazelle across the screen.

It’s fine that you live here, my brother says.

I’m serious, my brother says. Routine is good. I don’t mind checking on you. Giving you shit to do. Even if it is more for you than for me.

I don’t say anything to this. My brother burps, pulling from his beer.

But, he says, I think, you know, it isn’t every day that you have the chance to leave. And not just to leave but to have someone who wants you to follow them. That’s rare, you know. Doesn’t happen often.

How the fuck do you know that?

Your boyfriend mentioned it the other day. Before he took off.

Fuck, nigga. Got it. Maybe you should just stay here.

My shoulders tense up. I feel like I should stand. And also like I’m stuck. I take another mouthful of fried rice, allowing its flavors to settle across my tongue.

I don’t know what’ll happen if I leave, I say.

My brother bites his lip. He turns to me, snorting.

I said, So fucking what? You think anyone does? Don’t give everyone else so much credit. You have to move to find out where the motion will take you. It’s one thing to try without knowing, maybe. It’s another thing not to try at all. That’s for dumbasses. Or rich people.

My brother shakes his head. Then he reaches for my bowl, stacking his beer and his own bowl inside it, dropping them onto the kitchen counter, and stepping back into his shoes and leaving me again.

A few days later, I’m walking Carl through the airport when we run into this kid. The terminal’s crowded. Foot traffic flows around us.

The boy’s standing alone. Looking fucking lost. Holding a backpack in one hand and a Nintendo Switch in the other. He can’t be much older than five or six.

This kid looks up at the two of us, sniffling. Carl turns from him to me.

Should we say something? I ask.

Why the fuck are you asking me? Don’t you fucking work here?

I push the wheelchair over to the kid. He looks skeptical, wiping at his face. I ask where he’s supposed to be going, and he only sniffles back at me. He says something, and then something else, and Carl says, Japanese.

Carl glares at me. Then his gaze softens, settling into a face I didn’t even know he could make.

He speaks sweetly, slowly to the kid. Totally different voice.

The boy gawks. Then he says something, and then again.

Carl says something else to him. The kid manages to giggle.

Boy’s looking for his mother, Carl says. Went to the bathroom and lost her.

Shit, I say. Does he know what gate he’s flying out of?

Carl looks at me like I’m a fucking idiot.

You can’t be serious, he says.

You’re about to miss your flight, I say.

Then I’ll miss the flight, Carl says.

Carl sighs. Then he crosses his arms.

Shit happens, he says. Do you think you’re the only one who works at the airport? I will find my own way if that is what I have to do, because that is simply what I will have to do. We adjust. That’s life.

Realize that I’ve stopped breathing.

The kid looks from Carl to me and back, sniffling.

So I grab Carl’s wheelchair. And Carl talks to the kid, holding his hand as we walk.

The three of us step through the airport. From time to time, someone turns our way, but nobody stops us. We arrive at a screen displaying the departures and find one headed for Tokyo, and we walk there, halfway across the airport.

The kid chats and chats. Babbling. Carl answers occasionally, softly, full of warmth. We’ve nearly made it to the gate when we hear, all of a sudden, a shriek.

The kid’s mother sprints over. The father jogs behind her. She tugs on the kid’s arm, gently, while he smothers his nose into her shoulder, and this woman nods deeply to Carl before he waves her off.

She turns to me. I shake my head. She makes a face I can’t quite read, grinning, before she turns around. Their little family clusters together. Then the kid turns back to us, waving, and the trio rushes toward their boarding gate.

Which leaves Carl and me alone. Passengers start assembling beside the gate, tapping at their phones.

A thought forms for me, briefly. It disappears just as quickly.

But, as if he’s heard it, Carl clucks his tongue.

Why wouldn’t you? he says.

You heard me, Carl says.

And I think about that.

I will find my way, he says.

I give him a blank stare. Check my watch. And I look at Carl as I step to the flight counter. I look at him as the woman scrunches her nose, scans my voucher, flipping through my passport, shaking her head, until she says, Oh. There’s a seat. You really don’t have any bags?

No, I say. I really don’t.

I turn back to Carl.

Then, finally, as I step toward the tunnel, I wave.

And Carl, finally, waves back. ♦
