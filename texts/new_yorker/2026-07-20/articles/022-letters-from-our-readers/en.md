# Letters from Our Readers

Section: The Mail

Rubric: At a Price

Readers respond to Melanie Thernstrom’s piece about a concierge women’s-health doctor, Burkhard Bilger’s piece about the lone-star tick, and Adeline Goss’s piece about the potential connections between frontotemporal dementia and criminality.

It was hard for me to read Melanie Thernstrom’s piece about fabulously wealthy women spending thousands of dollars on a gynecologist who coaches them on optimizing their orgasms, because it arrived the same week that my town’s small community hospital, in southeastern Vermont, announced that it anticipates having to close its birthing center this year (“Billionaires’ Vagina Club,” July 6th & 13th). If that comes to pass, then many women will have to travel out of state, often across long distances, for obstetric care. This week, I also read that the average annual compensation for a C.E.O. of a major national health-insurance company is more than twenty million dollars. I also encountered a poll, no doubt instituted to mark our nation’s two-hundred-and-fiftieth birthday, asking: Are you proud to be an American? I’m thinking about how proud I would be if our country made serious strides to eliminate this level of economic injustice.

Karen Tyler Brattleboro, Vt.

Burkhard Bilger’s piece about how lone-star ticks are spreading alpha-gal syndrome, a condition that causes meat allergies, is terrifying and informative (“The Great Infestation,” July 6th & 13th). What’s troubling to me, besides the obvious, is the insistence on finding a way to continue to eat animals if your body starts to reject them. “Vegan” may be a curse word to some, but there’s never been a better, and easier, time to be one than now. Brands like Beyond and Impossible make delicious burger patties without meat. Of course, there are critics who say that these products don’t pass their purity tests, but alternative meats have the advantage of not requiring factory farming or breaking you out in a rash. Another plus is that you don’t have to raise cows or pigs only to kill them, just because you prefer the taste of red meat.

Robert Howard Madisonville, Ky.

Bilger’s article struck a chord with me. Fifty years ago, when I was thirty, I passed out with a swollen face and hives a few hours after eating prime rib at a celebratory dinner. My family doctor, in Tennessee, thought I had an allergic reaction, but he couldn’t tell what had triggered it, and implored me to see a specialist. All the allergy tests doctors later performed came back negative. It was only after I started eliminating foods that I realized my reactions were caused by red meat.

Many years later, I was diagnosed with alpha-gal syndrome. My journey with this condition has entailed visiting the emergency room three times, always carrying an EpiPen, travelling with a card that describes my allergy in several languages, and constantly alerting restaurant chefs and staff that I can have no food that might contain meat by-products or be contaminated by them. Last year, after a lovely dinner in San Francisco that I thought was safe, I was in an emergency room explaining alpha-gal to doctors who had never even heard of it.

I am waiting for the day when there might be a preventive remedy. In the meantime, I appreciate the exposure The New Yorker has given to this unusual allergy, which is prevalent in the South and now moving across the country.

Sarah Hunt Nashville, Tenn.

I was troubled by Adeline Goss’s recent article about Ray Howell, a doctor whose criminal actions may have been caused by frontotemporal dementia (Annals of Science, newyorker.com/goss-on-ray-howell, June 11th). Howell’s F.T.D. was linked to a genetic mutation known as a C9orf72 (C9) hexanucleotide repeat expansion. I write from a place of concern for the well-being of those living with this mutation, as I fear that many readers will be left with the impression that there is strong evidence that having it is definitively and consistently associated with profoundly negative characteristics, including criminality. As a statistician who has partnered with A.L.S./F.T.D. advocacy groups and researchers to interrogate the published literature on the genetically at-risk community, I believe that the evidence does not support this impression.

Goss cites a study that found half of F.T.D. patients who were C9-mutation carriers had fixed behavioral patterns, like rigid meal schedules and excessive exercise routines. Research indicates that the majority of mutation carriers will never develop F.T.D., however, so it is unclear how these findings would apply to the broader C9-carrier population. Furthermore, this study should not be used to draw conclusions because its sample size for carriers was very small: only twenty. The authors also describe exercising more than four times a week as excessive. Surely most people would agree that four times a week is not necessarily excessive. What’s more, the authors do not present any formal analysis for the observation, which is based on relatives’ accounts, that the study’s subjects became (as Goss quotes) “bad tempered” if they couldn’t exercise. Finally, the paper acknowledges that the study’s interviewer was not blind to the C9 status of its participants and went in with a specific hypothesis—circumstances that risk bias.

More broadly, it’s also worth remembering how far Dr. Howell’s actions are from the norm: even among C9 carriers who do develop F.T.D., serious crimes of this kind are rare. We should be careful how we communicate the implications of C9 mutations so as not to stigmatize carriers, or discourage them from participating in research or getting clinical care. C9 carriers deserve to be treated with respect—and to know that a sequence in their DNA does not define who they are.

Daniel Brickman San Francisco, Calif.
